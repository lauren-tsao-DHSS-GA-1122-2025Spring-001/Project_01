Ecce Homo

From a historical fresco to an internet sensation, Ecce Homo is a masterpiece in its own unique way, its botched restoration putting its small town in Spain on the radar of many tourists.

Around the year 1930, in a small Spanish town of Borja, a painter by the name of Elías García Martínez painted a fresco directly on the wall of the Santuario de Misericordia church as a gift.
Titled Ecco Homo, translated as 'Behold the Man', the fresco had the height and width of twenty and sixteen inches respectively. Its style echoed that of Italian artist Guido Reni's high-Baroque works.
The fresco had a very humble beginning. General opinion amongst the press about it was that it was "a work of little artistic importance" and that “Martínez is not a great artist and his painting Ecce Homo is not a ‘masterpiece'".
However, it became a form of sentimental value among the locals, originated from a shared passion for historical preservation, as well as appreciation and respect for the artwork.

In just 80 years however, Elías García Martínez’s Ecce Homo begin to fade, caused by moisture damage. As a result, much of the details of the painting has been compromised.
The sight of the deteoriated painting greatly troubled 80-year-old Cecilia Giménez, a parishioner of the church and amateur painter. With the blessing of the priest, Giménez put her artistic skills to the test, in a bid to restore the fresco to its former glory.
Unfortunately, the attempt backfired tremendously, and news of Giménez's failure was reported by the Spanish newspaper, Heraldo, on August 21, 2012. The article spread like wildfire throughout the internet, propelling the painting to fame.
The silver lining that came with this incident however, was that since the fresco's dramatic makeover, its once quiet town of Borja is now booming with tourism, attracting more than 150,000 tourists from around the globe.
